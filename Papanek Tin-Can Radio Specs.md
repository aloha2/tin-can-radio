# Papanek / Seeger Tin-Can Radio Specs

Reference note for the tin-can radio project.

## Identity

- **Object names:** `Tin Can Radio`, `Radio Receiver for the Third World`, sometimes `Dosenradio`.
- **Designers:** Victor Papanek with George Seeger / George Seegers.
- **Approx. period:** commonly referenced as early-to-mid `1960s`; sources variously cite `1962`, `1965`, and decorated examples around `1968–1971`.
- **Published context:** illustrated/discussed in Victor Papanek's `Design for the Real World` (`1971`).
- **Intended context:** very low-cost radio access for technically/economically underserved regions, especially where grid electricity and replacement batteries were not realistic.

## Material / component recipe

| Subsystem | Reported material / part | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Enclosure / body | Used `juice can` or `food tin` | The discarded can is both housing and symbolic material choice. |
| Fuel | `Paraffin wax` plus a `wick` | Often described as candle-like. |
| Alternative fuel | More wax, paper, dried cow dung, or other burnable material | Key point: user-refillable with locally available fuel. |
| Energy conversion | Heat-to-electricity element, commonly described as a `thermocouple` | Rising heat from flame produces enough power for the tiny receiver. |
| Receiver circuit | Extremely simple receiver; often described as `one-transistor`; some later descriptions mention a `tunnel diode` | Exact circuit details vary by source; treat this as a minimal nonselective receiver, not a modern tuned radio. |
| Audio output | Cheap `earplug` / `earpiece speaker` | Private low-power listening rather than loudspeaker output. |
| Antenna | Improvised / hand-woven `copper wire` antenna, sometimes described as radial halo | Visually one of the most distinctive parts. |
| Ground | Earth wire terminating in a used `nail` | Nail driven into ground acts as earth connection. |
| Tuning / selectivity | No normal tuner; `nonselective` receiver | Intended for places with only one strong local/state broadcast signal. |
| Packaging / delivery | Parts could be stowed inside the can | Some descriptions emphasize compact shipping/self-assembly. |

## Functional principle

1. User lights the wick/fuel.
2. Heat rises from the flame.
3. A heat-to-electricity element converts the temperature difference into a tiny electrical output.
4. That output powers a minimal radio receiver.
5. The receiver feeds a low-power earplug/earpiece.
6. Antenna + ground improve signal capture.

## Cost claim

- Frequently cited manufacturing cost: about **`9 US cents`** on a cottage-industry basis, using `1960s` cost assumptions.
- Treat the number as a historical/design-rhetorical claim, not a current BOM estimate.

## Design meaning / takeaway

The important lesson is not that this is a technically good radio by modern standards. It is a concentrated example of Papanek's values:

- ultra-low material cost;
- repairability and local assembly;
- reused waste material;
- operation without batteries or grid power;
- user-refillable fuel;
- function prioritized over conventional industrial-design taste;
- intentionally suitable for cottage-industry production rather than centralized manufacturing.

## Caveats

- The exact circuit description is inconsistent across secondary sources (`one-transistor`, `tunnel diode`, `thermocouple-powered`, `nonselective receiver`).
- It would not behave like a useful modern multi-station radio because it lacks normal tuning/selectivity.
- The design reflects the development/political assumptions of its era and should be read critically, not copied uncritically.
